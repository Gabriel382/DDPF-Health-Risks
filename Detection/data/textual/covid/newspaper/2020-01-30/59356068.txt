Dow futures drop over 200 points as coronavirus fears overshadow upbeat earnings 
 U.S. stock futures fell Thursday, keeping pace with global markets as the coronavirus epidemic death toll and infections climbed, alongside concerns over how the outbreak could hurt the Chinese economy.

Those worries overshadowed positive earnings news from Microsoft Corp and Tesla Inc reported late Wednesday, while airline stocks American Airlines, Delta and United all fell in pre-market trade Thursday.

Futures on the Dow Jones Industrial Average YMH20, -0.76%  fell 234 points or 0.8%, to 28,476, while S&P 500 futures ESH20, -0.78%  dropped 26.65 points, or 0.8%, to 3,245.75. Nasdaq-100 futures NQH20, -0.64%  declined 62 points, or 0.7%, to 9,036.50.

On Wednesday, the Dow DJIA, +0.04%  eked out a gain of 11.60 points, or less than 0.1%, to end at 28,734.45, while the S&P 500 SPX, -0.09%  gave up 2.84 points, or 0.1%, to close at 3,273.40. The Nasdaq Composite COMP, +0.06%  finished at 9,275.16, gain of 5.48 points, or 0.1%.

Chinese authorities on Thursday said more than 7,700 people have been infected, with at least 170 dead. World Health Organization officials expressed “great concern” over the virus’s spread outside of China. A U.N. panel is expected to meet Thursday to weigh if the outbreak should be declared a global emergency.

Markets have been dogged by worries over the coronavirus epidemic this week, and a renewed selloff that started in Asia on Thursday spread to European markets and U.S. stock futures.

Taiwan stock-markets Y9999, -5.75% reopened after the Lunar New Year holiday to a nearly 6% loss, with Apple AAPL, +2.09%  supplier Foxconn tumbling nearly 10%. Hong Kong stocks HSI, -2.62%  dropped over 2%. The Stoxx Europe 600 index SXXP, -0.94%   retreated 0.9% to trade at 415.64.

China’s stock markets will remain closed until next Monday, but investors are apprehensive that the market will face substantial losses as investors there catch up to global concerns over the deadly virus. Worries are growing over the fallout to the country’s economy, as airlines in the U.S. and elsewhere have canceled flights to China. Starbucks SBUX, -2.12%  said Wednesday that it planned to raise guidance, but was stopped as the virus has forced it to shut half its stores in China.

“Investors are clearly looking to the spread of the coronavirus and hoping it doesn’t start to show up more in Europe and the U.S. Fed chair Jay Powell’s concern over the outbreak only underlined caution as the order of the day,” said Neil Wilson, chief market analyst at Markets.com.

Federal Reserve Chief Jerome Powell said Wednesday that the U.S. economy is in good shape, as the central bank left a key interest rate unchanged, but he said the Fed is closely monitoring the severity of the deadly coronavirus and potential disruption to the global economy.

Government statisticians take their first swing at estimating U.S. fourth-quarter gross domestic product at 8:30 a.m. Eastern. Economists surveyed by MarketWatch forecast economic growth to have slowed to a 1.9% annualized pace versus a 2.1% read in the third quarter.

Data on U.S. weekly jobless claims is also due at 8:30 a.m. Eastern. Economists expect first-time claims to rise to 218,000.

The yield on the 10-year U.S. Treasury note TMUBMUSD10Y, -1.04%   fell 3 basis points to 1.57%, around its lowest level since October 9 last year.

West Texas Intermediate crude for March delivery CLH20, -2.44%   on the New York Mercantile Exchange fell $1.23 cents, or 2.3%, to $52.10 a barrel. Gold futures rose on safe-haven demand on Wednesday. February gold GCG20, +0.60%   rose $9.4, or 0.6%, to $1,579.90 an ounce in electronic trading.

The U.S. dollar edged 0.1% lower against a basket of its rival currencies, according to the ICE U.S. Dollar index DXY, -0.08%
